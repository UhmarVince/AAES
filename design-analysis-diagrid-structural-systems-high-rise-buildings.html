Design and Analysis of Diagrid Structural Systems for High-Rise Buildings

Engr. Uhmar Vince Aceron
Principal Structural Engineer | June 14, 2026
A diagrid structural system, characterized by a perimeter network of intersecting diagonal members, represents an innovative approach to high-rise building design. This configuration integrates the structural frame with the building's facade, offering inherent stability and efficiency. Unlike conventional orthogonal frame systems, diagrids efficiently resist both vertical and lateral forces through axial action in their members. This inherent load path optimization leads to reduced material consumption and enhanced structural performance, making them a compelling choice for tall and supertall structures. The system's aesthetic qualities are also significant, allowing for column-free interiors and a distinctive architectural expression. The adoption of diagrid systems is driven by their demonstrated capacity to meet stringent performance criteria, including high stiffness-to-weight ratios and superior seismic and wind resistance. This document explores the fundamental mechanics, analytical methodologies, and critical design considerations essential for the successful implementation of diagrid systems in contemporary high-rise construction.

Fundamental Principles of Diagrid Structural Behavior

Structural Mechanics and Load Transfer

A primary characteristic of diagrid systems is their ability to resist shear forces primarily through axial action in the diagonal members. In conventional framed structures, lateral loads are resisted by bending in beams and columns, often necessitating deep members or shear walls. Diagrids, however, behave akin to a vertical truss or a hollow tube, where the diagonal elements act in tension and compression to transfer lateral forces directly to the foundation. Vertical loads are also effectively carried by the axial components of the diagonal members, distributing forces uniformly throughout the perimeter. This direct load transfer mechanism minimizes shear deformation and significantly enhances the building's lateral stiffness, contributing to superior performance under dynamic loading conditions. The global behavior of a diagrid structure under lateral loads can be understood as a cantilever beam, where the diagonal members act as web elements and the horizontal ring beams (if present) or floor diaphragms act as flanges, providing continuity and resisting differential axial forces.
  • Axial Force Dominance: Diagrid members primarily resist forces through axial tension and compression, leading to an efficient load path for both vertical and lateral loads.
  • Shear and Bending Resistance: Unlike traditional frames, diagrids resist shear forces directly through the axial components of their diagonals, while their global action as a cantilever resists overturning moments.
  • Enhanced Lateral Stiffness: The triangulated geometry provides inherent rigidity, minimizing lateral deflections and improving dynamic performance under wind and seismic events.
  • Material Efficiency: Focusing forces axially reduces flexural demands, optimizing member sizes and often leading to significant material savings compared to conventional framed systems.

Geometric Considerations and Optimization

The geometric configuration of a diagrid system significantly influences its structural performance and constructability. Optimization studies frequently focus on identifying the most efficient parameters.
  • Diagrid Angle Selection: This is a critical parameter. A steeper angle (closer to vertical) typically enhances resistance to overturning moments, while a shallower angle is more effective in resisting shear forces. The optimal angle often balances these aspects and is typically determined through detailed analysis based on the building's height-to-width ratio and predominant lateral load types. Common optimal ranges fall between 60 to 75 degrees from the horizontal.
  • Module Size and Repetition: The module height, defined as the vertical distance between horizontal intersections of diagonal members, impacts structural efficiency and architectural expression. Repetitive modules streamline fabrication and erection processes, leading to cost and time efficiencies.
  • Node Design and Detailing: The connections, or nodes, where multiple diagonal members converge, are paramount for effective force transfer and maintaining structural integrity. Their design must accommodate complex three-dimensional forces and ensure continuity of load paths.
  • Material Selection: The choice of materials, such as high-strength steel or concrete-filled steel tubes, plays a significant role in optimizing member sizes, enhancing overall efficiency, and achieving desired aesthetic qualities.

Analytical Approaches for Diagrid Systems

Linear Elastic Analysis

Initial design and preliminary sizing of diagrid systems typically involve linear elastic analysis. This approach assumes that materials behave adiabatically and deformations are small, allowing for straightforward application of structural mechanics principles.
  • Finite Element Modeling Techniques: FEM is the predominant tool for creating detailed computational models that accurately represent the complex geometry and connectivity. Careful consideration must be given to boundary conditions, joint connectivity, and member properties.
  • Approximations for Preliminary Design: For initial design iterations, simplified analytical models, such as equivalent truss or shear-flexural beam analogies, can provide reasonable estimates of global stiffness and force distribution, aiding in rapid assessment of different configurations.
  • Software Applications and Considerations: Commercial structural analysis software is extensively used. Engineers must ensure correct modeling practices for complex nodal interactions and overall system behavior.
  • Limitations: While providing fundamental understanding, linear elastic analysis has limitations in predicting post-elastic behavior, local buckling, or the ultimate capacity of the structure.

Advanced Nonlinear Analysis

For a comprehensive understanding of diagrid system performance, especially under extreme loading conditions, advanced nonlinear analysis is indispensable. This includes both geometric and material nonlinearity.
  • P-Delta Effects and Geometric Nonlinearity: Accounts for the influence of axial forces on the flexural stiffness of members, which is particularly critical in tall structures where significant lateral deflections can occur. Rigorous assessment of global and local stability is required.
  • Material Nonlinearity and Inelastic Behavior: Models the inelastic behavior of structural materials beyond their elastic limit. This is crucial for evaluating ductility, energy dissipation capacity, and ultimate strength, especially in seismic design where controlled yielding is expected.
  • Nonlinear Static and Dynamic Procedures: Nonlinear static procedures (pushover analysis) simulate the structural response under increasing lateral loads. Nonlinear dynamic procedures (time history analysis) apply ground motion records or dynamic forces directly, providing detailed simulation of time-dependent response, including hysteretic energy dissipation.
  • Progressive Collapse Assessment: Such advanced analyses are vital for assessing the resistance to progressive collapse, ensuring structural robustness even if a primary load-bearing element is compromised.

Dynamic Analysis and Performance Assessment

Diagrid systems, by virtue of their high stiffness, generally exhibit favorable dynamic characteristics. However, comprehensive dynamic analysis is critical for assessing their response to transient loads such as seismic events and wind gusts.
  • Modal Analysis and Vibration Characteristics: Performed to determine the natural frequencies and mode shapes, which are fundamental to understanding the structure's dynamic behavior and potential resonance conditions.
  • Response Spectrum and Time History Analysis for Seismic Loads: Response spectrum analysis is widely adopted for seismic design, using modal properties and a design response spectrum to estimate maximum member forces and displacements. For critical or unusually configured buildings, nonlinear time history analysis provides a more detailed and accurate simulation of time-dependent seismic response, including inelastic behavior, consistent with general provisions in NSCP 2015.
  • Wind Tunnel Testing for Aerodynamic Effects: High-rise diagrids frequently necessitate wind tunnel testing to obtain empirical data on aerodynamic forces, pressures, and dynamic responses. This data is crucial for refining the structural design, calibrating analytical models, and ensuring serviceability criteria related to occupant comfort and facade performance are met under various wind conditions.

Design Considerations and Challenges

Seismic Design Principles for Diagrids

Designing diagrid systems for seismic resilience requires a thorough understanding of their unique load transfer mechanisms and ductility requirements, aligning with established seismic provisions.
  • Ductility Requirements and Detailing: As outlined in relevant structural codes, including NSCP 2015, structures must resist prescribed ground motions with sufficient strength and ductility. For diagrids, this means ensuring critical regions, particularly nodal connections and diagonal members, possess adequate capacity for inelastic deformations without brittle failure.
  • Energy Dissipation Mechanisms: Proper detailing is paramount to ensure stable hysteretic behavior under cyclic loading and to prevent buckling of compression members, ensuring predictable energy dissipation.
  • Capacity Design Philosophy: Applied to ensure yielding occurs in designated ductile elements (e.g., diagonal members) while brittle elements (e.g., connections, foundations) remain elastic, consistent with seismic design principles for special moment or braced frames.
  • P-Delta Analysis: Crucial to account for geometric nonlinearities that may amplify seismic demands in tall structures, ensuring global stability under large deflections.

Wind Load Effects and Serviceability

High-rise diagrid structures are significantly influenced by wind loads, with serviceability often governing the design beyond strength considerations.
  • Mitigation of Wind-Induced Vibrations: The inherent stiffness of diagrids helps control wind-induced deflections. However, aerodynamic shaping and detailed wind tunnel testing are often employed to mitigate adverse wind effects, such as vortex shedding.
  • Drift Control and Lateral Stiffness: Design ensures that peak lateral deflections (drift) remain within acceptable limits to protect non-structural components and maintain overall structural integrity.
  • Comfort Criteria for Occupants: Serviceability involves ensuring peak accelerations at upper occupied floors remain within established criteria for occupant comfort under frequent wind events. Supplemental damping systems may be considered if required.

Connection Design and Constructability

The design of connections in diagrid systems presents significant engineering challenges due to the convergence of multiple diagonal members and potentially horizontal ring beams at nodes.
  • Node Detailing: These critical points require robust detailing to transfer complex three-dimensional forces and ensure load path continuity. Connection types range from bolted splices to fully welded assemblies, or custom cast nodes, each with specific fabrication and performance characteristics.
  • Fabrication and Erection Challenges: The constructability of nodes, including fabrication tolerances, ease of on-site assembly, and inspection requirements, must be considered early. Prefabrication of diagrid modules can enhance quality control and accelerate erection.
  • Tolerance Management: Precise planning for geometric tolerances is essential to ensure proper fit-up during construction and to avoid cumulative errors that could impact structural performance.
  • Construction Sequence Effects: Careful planning of the construction sequence, including temporary bracing and shoring, is necessary to maintain stability during the erection phase.

Fire Resistance and Protection

Ensuring adequate fire resistance is a critical aspect of diagrid structural design, particularly for externally exposed members.
  • Member Protection Strategies: External diagrid elements, often exposed to fire from within or adjacent structures, require specialized protection such as intumescent coatings, fire-resistant spray-on materials, or encasement in fire-rated concrete to maintain load-bearing capacity.
  • Performance-Based Fire Design: This approach involves sophisticated thermal and structural analysis to demonstrate that the structure can withstand expected fire scenarios, considering fire load, ventilation, and material thermal response. This ensures structural integrity for safe evacuation and fire suppression.
  • Code Compliance: Design must adhere to relevant fire safety codes and standards to ensure life safety and property protection.

Material Selection and Sustainability

The choice of materials significantly impacts performance, cost, and environmental footprint.
  • High-Strength Steel Applications: Common due to its excellent strength-to-weight ratio, enabling slender members and architectural transparency.
  • Concrete-Filled Steel Tubes (CFST): Increasingly utilized for enhanced fire resistance, improved buckling resistance, and increased stiffness.
  • Material Efficiency: Diagrids inherently offer optimized load paths, leading to reduced member sizes and lower embodied carbon footprints compared to more material-intensive conventional frames.
  • Sustainable Practices: Prefabrication and modular construction can reduce waste and improve resource efficiency. Life cycle assessment (LCA) tools can evaluate environmental impacts, guiding designers towards sustainable solutions, including the integration of recycled content and consideration of material recyclability.
The design and analysis of diagrid structural systems require a holistic approach, integrating advanced computational tools, a deep understanding of structural mechanics, and a keen awareness of constructability and performance requirements. As structures continue to push boundaries in height and complexity, diagrids offer a robust, efficient, and aesthetically striking solution, contributing significantly to resilient and sustainable high-rise construction.
