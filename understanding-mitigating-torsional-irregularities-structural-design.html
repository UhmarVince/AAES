Understanding and Mitigating Torsional Irregularities in Structural Design

Engr. Uhmar Vince Aceron
Principal Structural Engineer | May 05, 2026

Introduction to Torsional Irregularity in Structural Systems

Structural systems are meticulously designed to resist a multitude of applied loads, including sustained gravity loads, transient wind forces, and dynamic seismic events. The overarching objective is to maintain structural integrity, ensure long-term serviceability, and provide safety for occupants. A cornerstone of this design philosophy is the pursuit of predictable and uniform distribution of internal forces across all structural elements. However, a significant challenge arises from torsional irregularity, a critical condition where the structure's center of mass does not align with its center of rigidity. This fundamental eccentricity, particularly under lateral loading conditions, generates an inherent torsional moment. This moment subsequently induces differential lateral displacements throughout the structure's plan, leading to non-uniform stress distributions and potentially severe concentrations of forces in individual structural components. The imperative to understand, quantify, and effectively address torsional irregularity is heightened in seismically active regions, where its presence can drastically amplify a building's dynamic response and intrinsic vulnerability during an earthquake.The implications of unmitigated torsional irregularity are far-reaching. They include an uneven distribution of shear forces among the primary vertical load-resisting elements, placing disproportionate demands on perimeter elements, and increasing the susceptibility to brittle failure modes. Contemporary building codes, including NSCP 2015, explicitly define criteria for the identification and evaluation of torsional irregularities, thereby mandating rigorous analytical procedures and specific design considerations to ensure the attainment of acceptable performance levels. Consequently, successful structural design inherently demands a comprehensive grasp of this phenomenon, encompassing its multifarious causes, precise methods of identification, and robust strategies for mitigation. This whitepaper aims to systematically delineate these critical aspects, offering a technical exposition of torsional irregularity and its profound implications for achieving resilient and robust structural design.

Causes and Classification of Torsional Irregularity

Torsional irregularity within a structural system can originate from a diverse range of design choices, architectural demands, and construction practices. A proactive approach to identifying these underlying causes is essential for their prevention or effective management during the initial stages of design. Primary contributing factors typically include an asymmetric distribution of mass, irregular plan geometries, and a non-uniform distribution of lateral stiffness throughout the structure. Asymmetry in mass can result from various factors, such as the eccentric placement of heavy mechanical or electrical equipment, variations in floor finishes, or changes in functional occupancy loads across a given floor plate. Conversely, irregular plan shapes, such as 'L', 'T', 'U', or other complex configurations, almost inherently introduce a separation between the center of mass and the center of rigidity, particularly if the lateral force resisting elements are not strategically positioned to counteract these inherent eccentricities. Furthermore, discontinuities in the lateral stiffness system, manifest as variations in the dimensions or material properties of columns or walls, the presence of large openings in shear walls, or abrupt changes in building geometry through setbacks in the vertical elevation, can significantly shift the center of rigidity relative to the center of mass.
Building codes typically classify torsional irregularity based on the degree of eccentricity between the center of mass and the center of rigidity, providing quantitative metrics to assess potential severity and guide subsequent analysis requirements. A common method to classify a structure as torsionally irregular involves comparing the maximum story drift at an extreme point of the structure, perpendicular to the direction of the applied lateral force, with the average story drift of the two ends of the structure. If this ratio exceeds a predefined threshold (e.g., 1.2 or 1.4, as specified by relevant codes), the structure is categorized as torsionally irregular. This differential response is a clear indicator of an excessive rotational component in the structural deformation. The precise magnitude of this difference directly influences the required dynamic analysis procedures and specific design detailing provisions.
  • Asymmetric Mass Distribution: This refers to the uneven placement of dead and live loads across a floor plan, causing the overall center of mass for a particular level to deviate from the center of rigidity. Such imbalances generate an eccentric force application during lateral loading.
  • Irregular Plan Configuration: Buildings with non-rectangular or re-entrant corner plans (e.g., L-shaped, T-shaped, H-shaped) often exhibit inherent torsional tendencies. The geometry itself makes it challenging to achieve perfect alignment between mass and stiffness centers.
  • Discontinuous Lateral Stiffness: Variations in the stiffness of vertical load-resisting elements (e.g., shear walls, braced frames, moment frames) across the plan. This can be due to changes in material, section properties, or the presence of non-structural elements that inadvertently contribute to stiffness in an irregular manner.
  • Openings in Diaphragms: Large or eccentrically located openings in floor diaphragms can significantly reduce the diaphragm's in-plane stiffness and alter its ability to effectively transfer lateral forces, potentially exacerbating torsional effects by creating flexible diaphragm behavior.
It is incumbent upon structural engineers to proactively identify these potential sources of torsional irregularity during the conceptual and preliminary design phases. Close collaboration with architectural and other design disciplines is critical to develop integrated solutions that either minimize the eccentricity or effectively manage its adverse effects. Early detection and resolution are invariably more efficient and cost-effective than attempting modifications during later design stages or construction.

Consequences of Torsional Irregularity on Seismic Performance

The presence of torsional irregularity within a structure has profound and often detrimental implications for its performance when subjected to seismic loading. During an earthquake, ground motion imparts inertial forces at the center of mass of each floor diaphragm. In a torsionally irregular building, the inherent eccentricity between the center of mass and the center of rigidity directly translates into a significant torsional moment acting on the structure. This induced torsional moment results in a superimposed rotational response on the building's primary translational motion. The consequence is a substantial increase in lateral displacements and internal forces within specific structural elements, particularly those situated at the perimeter of the building and farthest from the center of rigidity.
The most significant consequence is the disproportionate amplification of forces and deformations in the lateral force resisting elements. Components located near the edges of the structure, especially those on the 'flexible' side (i.e., further from the center of rigidity in the direction of twisting), will experience considerably larger lateral displacements and internal shear forces compared to what would occur in a torsionally regular structure under identical loading. This highly unequal distribution of forces can lead to localized overstressing and premature, often brittle, failure of these critical elements, even if the overall building system was notionally designed to resist the total design lateral forces. The phenomenon of 'whipping' or excessive rotational distortion can induce inelastic deformations that were not adequately accounted for in a simplified, purely translational analysis.
Moreover, significant torsional responses can precipitate adverse soil-structure interaction effects. The differential settlements and rotations induced at the foundation level can propagate into additional stresses within both the foundation system and the overlying superstructure. For structures supported on deep foundations such as piles, this differential loading can lead to uneven pile demands and potential failure modes if the foundation system is not specifically designed to accommodate such rotational effects. The dynamic nature of seismic events further exacerbates these effects, as specific resonant torsional modes can be excited, leading to sustained and amplified rotational oscillations. This can be particularly problematic for the integrity and performance of non-structural elements, potentially causing extensive damage to architectural finishes, curtain walls, mechanical, electrical, and plumbing systems, thereby severely impacting the building's post-earthquake functionality, reparability, and economic viability. In the most severe scenarios, unmitigated torsional irregularity can initiate the progressive collapse of a structure, as localized demands exceed elemental capacities, triggering a cascade of failures. Building codes, such as NSCP 2015, are unequivocally explicit in their requirements for addressing torsional irregularity precisely because of these severe performance implications. The intent is to ensure that structures possess sufficient redundancy, strength, and ductility to accommodate the complex, non-uniform force distributions arising from torsional effects, thereby safeguarding life safety and minimizing both structural and non-structural damage.

Identification and Mitigation Strategies

Effective management of torsional irregularity is predicated upon its accurate and timely identification during the structural analysis and design phases. Building codes universally provide quantitative criteria for classifying a structure as torsionally irregular. A widely adopted method involves evaluating the ratio of the maximum story drift at an extreme point of the structure to the average story drift of the two ends of the structure, measured perpendicular to the direction of seismic loading. If this ratio exceeds a specified threshold, the structure is designated as torsionally irregular, thereby mandating more rigorous analysis and the application of specific design provisions. Dynamic analysis methods, such as response spectrum analysis or time-history analysis, are frequently required for such structures to accurately capture the intricate interaction of translational and rotational modes of vibration. These advanced analytical techniques provide a more realistic and detailed understanding of the forces and deformations compared to simplified equivalent static force procedures.
Mitigation strategies are primarily focused on two objectives: reducing the eccentricity between the center of mass and the center of rigidity, and enhancing the overall torsional stiffness and inherent redundancy of the structural system. Ideally, these strategies are conceptualized and integrated early in the architectural and structural design collaboration to preempt costly and complex modifications at later stages.
  • Symmetric Layout of Lateral Force Resisting Elements: The most effective approach involves arranging primary lateral force resisting elements (e.g., shear walls, braced frames, moment frames) symmetrically around the building's center of mass. This strategic placement directly minimizes the inherent eccentricity and ensures a more uniform distribution of stiffness.
  • Adjusting Mass Distribution: Modifying the architectural programming or the strategic placement of heavy equipment and utilities to achieve a more uniform distribution of mass across the floor plate. This aligns the center of mass more closely with the center of rigidity, reducing the torsional moment.
  • Increasing Torsional Stiffness: Enhancing the overall torsional resistance of the structure by incorporating additional shear walls, stiffening existing vertical elements, or employing coupled shear wall systems. Core wall systems in high-rise buildings are inherently effective in providing significant torsional stiffness.
  • Strategic Placement of Stiff Elements: Deliberately locating stiffer elements, such as concrete shear walls or robust braced frames, at the building's perimeter. These elements can act as effective 'torsional resisting couples' when subjected to eccentric loading, thereby counteracting the rotational tendencies.
  • Diaphragm Strengthening and Detailing: Ensuring that floor diaphragms are designed as sufficiently rigid and robust elements capable of efficiently transferring lateral forces to the vertical load-resisting system. This includes careful detailing of diaphragm connections to vertical elements and avoiding excessively large or eccentrically located openings that could compromise diaphragm integrity and stiffness.
  • Redundancy in Lateral Systems: Providing multiple, well-distributed lateral load paths increases the system's robustness against torsional effects. Redundant elements ensure that the failure of one component does not lead to disproportionate increase in demands on others, or to total structural instability.
  • Advanced Isolation and Dissipation Systems: For structures with pronounced irregularity, or those in regions of extreme seismicity, advanced techniques such as base isolation systems or the integration of supplemental energy dissipation devices (e.g., viscous dampers, yielding dampers) can be employed. While these systems do not directly eliminate eccentricity, they significantly reduce the overall seismic demands experienced by the structure, thereby mitigating the severe consequences of torsional responses by decoupling the structure from ground motion or by absorbing seismic energy.
When complete elimination of torsional irregularity is unfeasible due to unavoidable architectural or functional constraints, the structural design must explicitly account for the amplified forces and displacements. This necessitates a robust increase in the strength, stiffness, and ductility of critical structural elements, particularly those predicted to experience higher demands due to rotational effects. A detailed analysis, including the proper consideration of accidental torsion, becomes paramount to ensure that the design safely accommodates these amplified demands and maintains overall structural stability and life safety.

Design and Analysis Considerations for Torsionally Irregular Structures

Designing structures exhibiting inherent torsional irregularities mandates a comprehensive analytical and design approach that transcends the limitations of simplified equivalent static force procedures. The dynamic nature of seismic loads inherently leads to complex interactions between translational and torsional modes of vibration, resulting in highly non-uniform force distributions across the structural plan. Building codes, notably NSCP 2015, prescribe specific and more rigorous analytical procedures for torsionally irregular structures to ensure an adequate margin of safety and predictable performance under seismic events. These procedures almost universally necessitate the application of dynamic analysis methods.
A critical consideration in the analysis of both regular and irregular structures is the incorporation of accidental torsion. Even in theoretically symmetric structures, uncertainties inherent in mass distribution, variability in material and stiffness properties, and the unpredictable nature of seismic wave propagation can induce minor eccentricities. Building codes universally account for this by requiring the consideration of an additional, fictitious eccentricity, typically a specified percentage of the building's plan dimension perpendicular to the direction of force, to be applied in the analytical model. For structures already classified as torsionally irregular due to inherent eccentricity, this accidental torsion is applied in conjunction with the calculated inherent eccentricity, strategically chosen to produce the most critical torsional effect, thereby further amplifying the design forces and moments on certain elements.
The selection of an appropriate analysis method is fundamentally crucial. For structures classified as torsionally irregular, the equivalent static force procedure is often deemed insufficient as it cannot accurately capture the complex interaction of dynamic translational and rotational responses. Instead, response spectrum analysis is a commonly required dynamic method. This procedure accounts for multiple modes of vibration, including significant torsional modes, and combines their peak effects using statistical methods such as the Square Root of the Sum of Squares (SRSS) or the Complete Quadratic Combination (CQC) rule to estimate the most probable peak responses. For structures with pronounced irregularities, or those located in very high seismic zones, nonlinear time-history analysis may be mandated. This advanced analytical method utilizes actual ground motion records (accelerograms) and meticulously accounts for the nonlinear, inelastic behavior of structural materials and components. It provides a highly refined and realistic understanding of the structure's performance under extreme loading conditions, allowing for detailed assessment of inelastic deformations, energy dissipation capacities, and potential failure mechanisms. This level of analysis is invaluable for critical or uniquely irregular structures.
Furthermore, exceptional attention must be dedicated to the detailing of structural elements within torsionally irregular buildings. Elements that are predicted to experience amplified forces and deformations due to torsional effects require enhanced detailing for both strength and ductility. This includes implementing robust confinement reinforcement in columns and beam-column joints, ensuring adequate development length and anchorage of all reinforcing bars, and designing exceptionally strong and ductile connections between all primary structural elements. Floor diaphragms must also be designed and detailed to act as sufficiently rigid or semi-rigid elements, capable of efficiently transferring amplified shear forces to the vertical lateral load-resisting system without experiencing excessive in-plane deformations that could exacerbate torsional effects. Detailing around diaphragm openings and re-entrant corners is particularly critical.
The interaction between the superstructure and the foundation system represents another pivotal aspect. Torsional effects can induce significant differential forces, moments, and settlements in the foundation, necessitating a meticulously engineered design of footings, pile caps, and grade beams to accommodate these complex demands. Soil-structure interaction (SSI) effects should be rigorously considered, especially for structures founded on soft or liquefiable soils, as the rotational stiffness and damping characteristics provided by the underlying soil can profoundly influence the dynamic response of the entire structural system. Neglecting these intricate interactions can lead to unconservative foundation designs and potential distress to the substructure. Additionally, the overall robustness and redundancy of the structural system should be carefully evaluated. In torsionally irregular structures, the reliance on a few perimeter elements to resist the bulk of torsional shear can reduce redundancy. Design should aim to distribute stiffness and strength more uniformly or ensure that critical elements have substantial overstrength to prevent premature failure.
In conclusion, torsional irregularity presents a fundamental and significant challenge in structural engineering, possessing the clear potential to severely compromise the seismic performance, serviceability, and ultimate safety of buildings. Through a thorough and proactive understanding of its complex causes, accurate quantitative identification utilizing appropriate analytical tools, and the judicious application of robust mitigation strategies alongside advanced analytical techniques, structural engineers can confidently design and deliver resilient structures. These structures will meet and exceed the rigorous demands stipulated by modern building codes and effectively safeguard against the intricate and potent forces imposed by severe natural hazards. Continuous professional development and adherence to best practices are essential for navigating these complex design scenarios.
